# Stage 3c - Contribution Potential and Misconception Check

## Function

Discuss with the LLM the possible contribution of the candidate directions produced in Stage 3b and identify conceptual, terminological, evidential, or methodological misconceptions.

The aim is to imagine what each direction could open, without treating that potential as confirmed.

## Evidence input

- Evidence package from Stage 2.
- Latent state mapping from Stage 3a.
- Candidate directions from Stage 3b.
- Human corrections and decisions accumulated so far.
- Reporting rule: keep the record traceable to the LLM output, combine short verbatim quotation with synthesis where useful, and reserve discussion of meaning or value for the later discussion/consolidation stage.

## Guiding question

What conceptual, methodological, technological, empirical, or organisational contributions could emerge from each candidate direction, and what misconceptions must be blocked before consolidation?

## Methodological restrictions

- The LLM may suggest possibilities, not confirm validity.
- Identify claims to avoid.
- Mark when a direction only repackages previous work.
- Separate conceptual, empirical, technical, organisational, and merely terminological novelty.
- Specify what data, artefacts, literature review, or specialist judgement would be required.
- If the LLM frames an appraisal as a "criterion", record it as the LLM's formulation, not as a human criterion or systematic procedure.
- Do not turn contribution potential into demonstrated contribution.
- Identify when the output merely repeats Stage 3b in new wording instead of adding substantive clarification.

## Suggested prompt

```text
Using the evidence package, the Stage 3a map, the Stage 3b candidate directions, and the human corrections provided, examine the contribution potential of each candidate direction.

For each direction, identify:
1. potential conceptual contribution;
2. potential methodological contribution;
3. potential technological contribution;
4. potential empirical contribution;
5. potential organisational or team-level contribution;
6. current evidence in the team's trajectory;
7. data, artefacts, literature, or expertise still required;
8. feasibility with the team's existing trajectory;
9. risk of overclaiming;
10. misconceptions, terms, or claims to avoid.

Do not present potential as demonstrated contribution. Make uncertainty visible.
```

## Record

### Date and time

Record when the interaction took place or when the synthesis was produced. Include timezone if this matters for traceability.

### Model, version, and parameters

Identify the LLM, product, model name or version, and any visible settings such as temperature, reasoning mode, tools, retrieval sources, or system constraints.

### Literal prompt

Paste the exact prompt given to the LLM. If Stage 3a maps, Stage 3b candidates, or human corrections were attached, name them and point to their stored location.

### LLM response or structured synthesis

Preserve the full response, or write a structured synthesis if the full transcript is stored elsewhere. Keep contribution claims as model output until researchers appraise them.

### Relevant verbatim excerpt

Copy only short excerpts that clarify a potential contribution, a warning, a misconception, or a claim that should be avoided.

### Human interpretation

Record whether the proposed contribution is meaningful, already present in the team's work, weakly grounded, overstated, or useful as a prompt for further inquiry.

### Human decisions

Record what should move to human consolidation, what should be reformulated, and what should be discarded or deferred.

### Limits identified

Record limits in the proposed contributions, evidence requirements, feasibility, terminology, model assumptions, or remaining need for literature and expert judgement.
